Why the Housing Market Feels Stuck

The U.S. housing market is not collapsing or fully recovering. It is stuck in a squeeze shaped by mortgage-rate lock-in, limited inventory, and affordability pressure.

What This Story Covers

Sales activity, inventory lock-in, price pressure, first-time buyer strain, and the policy debate shaping 2026.

A sign goes up early in the week and comes down just days later. No deal struck, no offer accepted. By October 2025, that scene played out 27 times for every 100 new listings [Realtor.com]. Across the country, homeowners stepped to the edge of the market, looked down, and stepped back.

The American housing market isn't crashing or correcting. It's just stuck. Many owners are staying put because selling means surrendering an unusually cheap mortgage, and many first-time buyers are shut out by today's price and rate math. If you've been wondering why the so-called American Dream of owning a home feels further away than ever, you're not imagining it.

A Market Frozen in Plain Sight

When the final tally came in for 2025, it confirmed what buyers had already felt all year: only 4.061 million existing homes sold [Realtor.com], coming in just 1,000 below 2024 and marking the lowest annual total since 1995. Compared with the 2013 to 2019 norm shown in the chart, that is roughly 1.1 million fewer sales than a healthier market used to produce.

For years, the housing market moved steadily forward. Then the 2008 financial crisis brought it to a halt. A slow recovery followed, until the pandemic created a new obstacle the market has yet to overcome.

2025 was another tough year for homebuyers, marked by record-high home prices and the kind of sales volumes this industry hasn't seen since the Clinton administration.

Lawrence Yun, Chief Economist, National Association of Realtors

Here's the strange part: while fewer homes were selling, the ones that did sell kept getting more expensive. The annual median hit $414,400, up 1.7% from 2024 [Realtor.com], continuing a streak of price increases that has lasted over a decade. Fewer buyers. Fewer sellers. Higher prices. It is a contradiction that only makes sense once you see how little inventory is actually available.

Golden Handcuffs

To understand why the market froze, you need to understand what happened with mortgage rates. During the pandemic, the Federal Reserve slashed interest rates to near zero to keep the economy alive. Millions of Americans took advantage, buying or refinancing homes at rates as low as 2.5% to 3%. At the time, it felt like winning the lottery. Lock in that rate, and your monthly payment is set for 30 years.

Then inflation hit. To fight it, the Fed raised rates faster than it had in four decades. By 2023, a standard 30-year mortgage had climbed past 7%, more than double what many homeowners were already paying.

Here's where it gets interesting. Selling your home doesn't just mean moving. It means giving up your rate. And once you understand that, everything else makes sense.

Imagine you bought a home in 2021 for $350,000 with a 3% mortgage. Your monthly payment (principal + interest) is about $1,476.

Now imagine you want to move to a slightly bigger home today, say $400,000. Same neighborhood, one extra bedroom. Sounds reasonable, right?

At today's 7% rate, that new mortgage costs you $2,661 per month, nearly $1,200 more every single month, for a home that isn't dramatically different from the one you already own.

That's $14,400 extra per year. For most families, that's a car payment, a vacation, a year of groceries.

Most people simply won't do it. And that's exactly why no one is moving.

Roughly 68.6% of mortgage-holding homeowners are locked in at 5% or below, and 51.5% are at 4% or below [Realtor.com]. These aren't stubborn people, they are making a completely rational decision. Economists call it the lock-in effect, and right now it is one of the biggest reasons homes are not hitting the market. The result: only 1.18 million unsold homes available nationwide [Realtor.com], nearly 820,000 fewer than what was normal before the pandemic.

The Affordability Wall

So what about people who don't already own a home, specifically the first-time buyers? Unfortunately, the picture is even harder for them.

Researchers at Harvard calculated that to comfortably afford a median-priced home, a buyer would need to earn at least $126,700 a year [Harvard JCHS]. That assumes a 20% down payment and spending no more than 30% of income on housing, the standard definition of affordable. The latest U.S. Census figure for median household income is $83,730 in 2024 [U.S. Census].

Think of it this way: a teacher earning $55,000 a year, a nurse making $72,000, a young couple both working full-time and bringing in $95,000 combined, none of them can comfortably afford the average American home right now.

This isn't a problem for the poor. It's a problem for the middle class.

The average American family can no longer afford the average American home.

Renters are not spared either. In Harvard's latest national housing tally, 22.6 million renter households spent more than 30% of their income on housing, and the number of apartments renting for under $600 a month had fallen 28% over the previous decade [Harvard JCHS]. The cheapest options are disappearing. They are not being replaced.

The Flight to Refuge

So people do what people have always done when a place gets too expensive: they move somewhere cheaper. Grand Rapids, Cleveland, Milwaukee, Pittsburgh, and Buffalo have all shown up in Realtor.com's refuge-market analysis [Realtor.com]. These are metros where prices were still roughly 20% to 30% below the national median, giving buyers at least some path back to affordability.

But here's the catch, and it's a significant one. The moment a place becomes known as affordable, demand floods in. And the moment demand floods in, affordability starts to erode. Realtor.com's refuge-market report showed some of the fastest price-per-square-foot growth landing in exactly these smaller, traditionally cheaper metros.

Rising delistings and the growth of refuge markets capture the push and pull defining today's housing market. Higher rates and years of rapid price growth have rewritten the rules of engagement for both buyers and sellers.

Danielle Hale, Chief Economist, Realtor.com

A Glimmer of Thaw

Here is some genuinely good news: the end of 2025 showed signs of life. Mortgage rates peaked at 7.04% in January [Realtor.com] and eased to 6.15% by the end of December [Freddie Mac]. It does not sound like much, but the market felt it immediately. Home sales jumped 5.1% in December alone [Realtor.com], the best single-month performance in nearly three years.

That kind of quick response tells you something important: the buyers are still out there. Millions of people want to own a home. They've just been waiting for the math to make a little more sense. When it did, even slightly, they moved.

Forecasters did not expect mortgage rates to snap back to pandemic lows in 2026. Realtor.com's December 3, 2025 forecast put the average 30-year mortgage rate around 6.3% for 2026 [Realtor.com]. That would not fix everything. But it would at least keep borrowing costs closer to the high-6% range than the 7% peaks buyers had been fighting.

The Policy Question

Washington has noticed, though the solutions being floated have not inspired much confidence. The Trump administration floated a 50-year mortgage in November 2025 [AP] and in January 2026 directed the government to buy $200 billion in mortgage bonds in an effort to push rates down [AP].

Neither idea generated much enthusiasm from economists. A 50-year mortgage lowers your monthly bill, but you end up paying far more in total interest. It just moves the pain further down the road. The bond-buying plan would have limited effect on rates that are ultimately set by global financial markets, well beyond any one government's control.

As for forecasts, the experts genuinely disagree. Lawrence Yun at NAR projected a 14% jump in existing-home sales for 2026 [NAR]. Fannie Mae's September 2025 outlook implied roughly 9.3% total home-sales growth from 2025 to 2026 [Fannie Mae]. Zillow projected a 4.3% increase in existing-home sales [Zillow], Redfin projected 3% [Redfin], and Realtor.com forecast 1.7% [Realtor.com]. The spread is not just uncertainty. It is a sign that nobody has a clear answer right now.

The Human Cost

Behind all these numbers are real people making hard calls. More than one in four U.S. households is now headed by someone 65 or older [U.S. Census], and many older owners are staying put in homes that are too big for them because moving makes no financial sense. Meanwhile, younger Americans are putting life on hold. Living with parents longer. Signing another lease. Watching friends do the same.

Think about what homeownership used to mean: you buy a starter home in your late 20s, build equity over time, upgrade when your family grows, and eventually retire with a paid-off asset. That was the ladder. That was how millions of middle-class families built wealth across generations.

For a growing number of Americans today, the first rung of that ladder is simply out of reach.

It's not that the dream has changed. It's that the math stopped working.

The 2025 housing market isn't broken in one way. It's broken in several at once. Years of underbuilding. A sudden rate shock. Surging inflation. Policies that addressed the symptoms but not the supply. The consequences, long deferred, are now falling hardest on the people who could least afford to wait.
