The Great American Freeze: How the Housing Market Got Stuck

Sales at a thirty-year low. Prices at a record high. Millions locked in by golden-handcuff mortgages, and millions more locked out entirely. Here's what's really going on, and why it affects you.

C
Cedric
Published November 5, 2025  ·  9 min read  ·  Housing & Economy

A sign goes up early in the week and comes down just days later. No deal struck, no offer accepted. Last October 2025, that scene played out 27 times for every 100 new listings [Realtor.com]. Across the country, homeowners stepped to the edge of the market, looked down, and stepped back.

The American housing market isn't crashing or correcting. It's just stuck. Nobody is selling because they can't afford to, and nobody under 40 can realistically afford to buy. If you've been wondering why the so-called "American Dream" of owning a home feels further away than ever, you're not imagining it.

The Numbers Don't Lie

When the final tally came in for 2025, it confirmed what buyers had already felt all year: only 4.06 million existing homes sold [NAR], tying 2024 as the worst annual total since 1995, and falling 1.1 million units short of what a healthy market looks like. The chart below tells the full story.

For years, the housing market moved steadily forward. Then the 2008 financial crisis brought it to a halt. A slow recovery followed, until the pandemic created a new obstacle the market has yet to overcome.

2025 was another tough year for homebuyers, marked by record-high home prices and the kind of sales volumes this industry hasn't seen since the Clinton administration.

Here's the strange part: while fewer homes were selling, the ones that did sell kept getting more expensive. The national median hit $414,400, up 1.7% from 2024 [NAR], continuing a streak of price increases that has lasted over a decade. Fewer buyers. Fewer sellers. Higher prices. It's a contradiction that shouldn't exist, unless the usual rules no longer apply.

Golden Handcuffs

To understand why the market froze, you need to understand what happened with mortgage rates. During the pandemic, the Federal Reserve slashed interest rates to near zero to keep the economy alive. Millions of Americans took advantage, buying or refinancing homes at rates as low as 2.5% to 3%. At the time, it felt like winning the lottery. Lock in that rate, and your monthly payment is set for 30 years.

Then inflation hit. To fight it, the Fed raised rates faster than it had in four decades. By 2023, a standard 30-year mortgage had climbed past 7%, more than double what many homeowners were already paying.

Here's where it gets interesting. Selling your home doesn't just mean moving. It means giving up your rate. And once you understand that, everything else makes sense.

Imagine you bought a home in 2021 for $350,000 with a 3% mortgage. Your monthly payment (principal + interest) is about $1,476.

Now imagine you want to move to a slightly bigger home today, say $400,000. Same neighborhood, one extra bedroom. Sounds reasonable, right?

At today's 7% rate, that new mortgage costs you $2,661 per month, nearly $1,200 more every single month, for a home that isn't dramatically different from the one you already own.

That's $14,400 extra per year. For most families, that's a car payment, a vacation, a year of groceries.

Most people simply won't do it. And that's exactly why no one is moving.

Nearly 69% of U.S. homeowners with a mortgage are locked in at 5% or below. Just over half are at 4% or below [FHFA]. These aren't stubborn people, they're making a completely rational decision. Economists call it the "lock-in effect," and right now it's the single biggest reason homes aren't hitting the market. The result: only 1.18 million unsold homes available nationwide [NAR], nearly 820,000 fewer than what was normal before the pandemic.

The Affordability Wall

So what about people who don't already own a home, specifically the first-time buyers? Unfortunately, the picture is even harder for them.

Researchers at Harvard calculated that to comfortably afford a median-priced home today, a buyer would need to earn more than $126,000 a year [Harvard JCHS]. That assumes a 20% down payment and spending no more than 30% of income on housing, the standard definition of "affordable." The median U.S. household income? About $78,000 [U.S. Census].

Think of it this way: a teacher earning $55,000 a year, a nurse making $72,000, a young couple both working full-time and bringing in $95,000 combined, none of them can comfortably afford the average American home right now.

This isn't a problem for the poor. It's a problem for the middle class.

The average American family can no longer afford the average American home.

The state of the nation's housing is in distress. The gap between what people earn and what shelter costs has become a structural feature of American life, not a temporary dislocation.

Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies — State of the Nation's Housing, 2025

And renters aren't spared either. A record 22.6 million renter households now spend more than 30% of their income just on housing [Harvard JCHS]. Meanwhile, the number of apartments renting for under $600 a month has fallen 28% over the past decade [Harvard JCHS]. The cheap options are disappearing. They're not being replaced.

The Flight to Refuge

So people do what people have always done when a place gets too expensive: they move somewhere cheaper. Grand Rapids, Michigan. Cleveland, Ohio. Columbus. Indianapolis. These Midwestern cities have quietly become magnets for families priced out of New York, Los Angeles, and Boston. Homes there run 20-30% below the national median. You can still buy something reasonable on a normal salary.

But here's the catch, and it's a significant one. The moment a place becomes known as "affordable," people flood in. And the moment people flood in, it stops being affordable. These refuge cities are now seeing the fastest price growth in the entire country, measured by price per square foot. The people escaping high costs are becoming the very force driving costs up somewhere else.

Rising delistings and the growth of refuge markets capture the push and pull defining today's housing market. Higher rates and years of rapid price growth have rewritten the rules of engagement for both buyers and sellers.

Danielle Hale — Chief Economist, Realtor.com

A Glimmer of Thaw

Here's some genuinely good news: the end of 2025 showed signs of life. Mortgage rates, which had been stuck above 7% for most of the year, quietly dropped to 6.15% by December [Freddie Mac]. It doesn't sound like much, but the market felt it immediately. Home sales jumped 5.1% in December alone [NAR], the best single-month performance in nearly three years.

That kind of quick response tells you something important: the buyers are still out there. Millions of people want to own a home. They've just been waiting for the math to make a little more sense. When it did, even slightly, they moved.

Most economists expect rates to stay above 6% through 2026. That won't fix everything. But it's a start.

There's quite sizeable pent-up demand. The only way to release it is more inventory and better affordability. Both need to move — not one, not the other. Both.

Lawrence Yun — Chief Economist, National Association of Realtors

The Policy Question

Washington has noticed, though the solutions being floated haven't inspired much confidence. The Trump administration proposed a 50-year mortgage (spread payments over half a century to lower monthly costs) and a $200 billion program to buy mortgage bonds and push rates down.

Neither idea generated much enthusiasm from economists. A 50-year mortgage lowers your monthly bill, but you end up paying far more in total interest. It just moves the pain further down the road. The bond-buying plan would have limited effect on rates that are ultimately set by global financial markets, well beyond any one government's control.

As for forecasts: the experts genuinely disagree. Lawrence Yun at NAR projects a 14% jump in home sales next year [NAR]. Others see gains of just 1.7% to 9% [Fannie Mae / Zillow / Redfin]. The wide range isn't just uncertainty. It's a sign that nobody has a clear answer right now.

The Human Cost

Behind all these numbers are real people making hard calls. Older homeowners, who now lead 28% of all U.S. households [U.S. Census], are staying put in homes that are too big for them, not because they want to, but because moving makes no financial sense. Meanwhile, younger Americans are putting life on hold. Living with parents longer. Signing another lease. Watching friends do the same.

Think about what homeownership used to mean: you buy a starter home in your late 20s, build equity over time, upgrade when your family grows, and eventually retire with a paid-off asset. That was the ladder. That was how millions of middle-class families built wealth across generations.

For a growing number of Americans today, the first rung of that ladder is simply out of reach.

It's not that the dream has changed. It's that the math stopped working.

The 2025 housing market isn't broken in one way. It's broken in several at once. Years of underbuilding. A sudden rate shock. Surging inflation. Policies that addressed the symptoms but not the supply. The consequences, long deferred, are now falling hardest on the people who could least afford to wait.
